What We Built

A Comparative NLP Study

We trained and benchmarked four models across classical ML, deep learning, and transformer architectures.

DistilBERT Transformer

Fine-tuned on 44K+ news articles. Uses self-attention to understand deep contextual meaning — the best performer in our study.

Bi-LSTM Deep Learning

Reads articles in both forward and backward directions using GloVe embeddings to capture word order and narrative flow.

Classical ML Baselines

Naive Bayes and SVM trained on TF-IDF, Empath, and hand-crafted linguistic features for robust comparison.

Anti-Leakage Design

Metadata tags (e.g. "Reuters") were stripped from training data to prevent shortcuts and ensure genuine generalization.

Research Overview

About This Project

A comparative NLP study evaluating machine learning, deep learning, and transformer-based approaches for automated fake news detection.

What is Fake News?

Fake news refers to deliberately fabricated or misleading information intended to deceive readers and influence public opinion. The rapid growth of digital platforms has made fake news one of the most pressing challenges of the information age — it spreads faster than corrections and can cause real-world harm.

Automated detection is critical because manual fact-checking cannot scale to the volume of content published online every day. NLP models like DistilBERT can analyze textual patterns at speed and scale no human team could match.

About DistilBERT

DistilBERT is a compact, faster version of Google's BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers) language model. Think of it as an AI that has read millions of web pages and learned how language works — it understands not just individual words, but the meaning formed by words in context.

For fake news detection, we fine-tuned DistilBERT on our specific dataset, teaching it the subtle linguistic differences between credible journalism and fabricated content — things like sensationalist phrasing, emotional manipulation, and lack of factual grounding.
